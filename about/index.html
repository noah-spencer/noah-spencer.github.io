Originally from Morgantown, West Virginia, I received my BS in Biology from West Virginia University in 2021. It was here that, through my work on tsetse flies under Dr. Rita Rio, I was introduced to the study of endosymbiosis — cells living stably inside of other cells. Shortly thereafter, I left for Arizona to work in John McCutcheon's lab where I study the Hodgkinia cicadicola endosymbionts of cicadas.

As much as I love trudging around with an insect net and conjuring up shell scripts in GNU Emacs, I was drawn to science less as a naturalist or as a computer geek and more as a reader, writer, and performer. Biology, being both a quantitative and an historical science, was particularly appealing to this sort of inner poet because it seemed to involve so many great stories — ones in which improbable outcomes not only occur but have long-lasting and wide-reaching consequences.

A mature physicist, acquainting himself for the first time with the problems of biology, is puzzled by the circumstance that there are no 'absolute phenomena' in biology. Everything is time-bound and space-bound. The animal or plant or micro-organism he is working with is but a link in an evolutionary chain of changing forms, none of which has any permanent validity.

—Max Delbrück, A Physicist Looks at Biology
